# How to use Mind-Mapper for learning success

*A 5-minute guide for students, researchers, autodidacts, and anyone
who reads with a pen in hand.*

---

## Why mind-maps work for learning

The brain stores knowledge as a network, not as a list. When you read a
chapter, your mind already builds a private spider-diagram of cause,
contrast, example, and counter-example — *we just don't usually see it.*

A mind-map is the externalised version. It's slower than highlighting and
faster than re-reading. The best students have always done it; what's
new is that you can now do it without redrawing the whole page every
time you change your mind.

> **The point of a mind-map is not to look pretty. It's to make the
> structure of an idea visible to your future self.**

---

## The 3-pass method

Most learners try to make a perfect map on the first read. Don't.

**Pass 1 — Skim and seed.** Open the PDF in the Reader. Drag-select the
chapter title and a one-line summary of each subheading. Send each one
to the map as a node. *Don't worry about hierarchy yet.* By the end of
pass 1 you should have the skeleton of the chapter — maybe 6–12 floating
ideas.

**Pass 2 — Read and connect.** Now read properly. When a sentence
*explains* a node from pass 1, drag it onto that node as a child.
When two nodes feel related, hold Shift, click both, and pick "Join with
line". You're not memorising — you're discovering the structure.

**Pass 3 — Compress and explain.** A week later, open the map without
the book. Try to teach yourself the chapter by walking each branch out
loud. When you stumble, you've found a gap. *That gap is your real
study list.* Add a sticky note where you got stuck.

This is the **Feynman technique** plus **spaced retrieval** — the two
techniques cognitive science consistently finds beat note-taking and
re-reading. Mind-Mapper just makes the geometry of it visual.

## Why local-first matters for learners

The maps you make while studying contain your raw thinking — what you
*don't* understand, what you confused, what you finally got. That is
the most personal artefact in your knowledge life. Mind-Mapper saves
every map locally in your browser by default. We never see them. You
can mirror them to your own Google Drive, Dropbox, or Zotero with one
click — but only when you decide.

---

## Three practical tips students use

1. **One PDF → one map.** Don't try to map a whole textbook at once.
   Per-chapter maps stay readable and merge cleanly later via "Group
   as branch."
2. **Sticky notes for confusion.** Drop a yellow sticky on any node
   you're unsure about. The Reminders page (`/output`) collects them so
   you can come back later.
3. **Highlight first, organise second.** Don't fight the structure on
   the first read. Capture loosely, organise on pass 2. The map should
   feel slightly chaotic during reading and clean after.

Mind-Mapper has an AI Research Assistant that can deepen any branch,
suggest cross-references, and rewrite a sub-tree as a polished essay.
**The AI runs on your own API key**, never ours. Two reasons:

- **You only pay for what you use.** A heavy week of research costs
  cents on Claude / OpenAI / Gemini. We don't mark it up.
- **Your reading stays private.** Your chapters never pass through our
  servers. Whatever you ask the AI goes from your browser straight to
  your provider.

You can paste a key in Settings → Add AI key. The fuel-gauge chip in
the top-right shows your remaining capacity at a glance.

## Build a habit

Open one PDF a day. Make a tiny map — even 5 nodes. After a month, you
will have a private, searchable galaxy of everything you read. Open
`/galaxy` to see it.
